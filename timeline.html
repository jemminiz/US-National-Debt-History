1776

According to the National Treasury, "The history of the Department of the Treasury began in the turmoil of the American Revolution, when the Continental Congress at Philadelphia deliberated the crucial issue of financing a war of independence against Great Britain." Since the government could not levy taxes or gather money from other governments, there was much turmoil as to how to raise money for fighting Great Britain. To solve this issue, the delegates decided to promise money that they did not yet have to the colonies.


Source: U.S. National Treasury →

1781

In 1781, the value of the United States dollar decreased very quickly, "at a rate of 500 to 1000 to 1 to hard currency" (United States National Treasury). After many protests, Robert Morris was designated the Superintendent of Finance in 1781. He balanced the federal budget and restored stability to the nation's finances. He was nicknamed "the Financier" because of his reputation for procuring goods or funds at a moment's notice. His staff included many people, such as a Comptroller, Treasurer, Register, and auditors. They controlled the nation's debt until 1784, when Morris had to resign due to illness. A treasury board continued to oversee budget until 1789.


Source: U.S. National Treasury →

1789

In 1789, the United States' Government switched to the United States Constitution. The First Congress was called to convene in New York in March of 1789. On September 2nd, Congress created a permanent institution for the management of government finances, known as the Department of Treasury. Alexander Hamilton served as the first Secretary of the Treasury, and did much with the United States' finances. Much to Congress' surprise, he insisted upon taking the country's war debt and paying it back dollar for dollar. This allowed the country to regain public credit. Hamilton also created things like the United States Bank, which does not exist anymore but used to act as the government's fiscal agent.


Source: U.S. National Treasury →

1814

In 1814, the United States Senate had to meet in different "emergency quarters" because the Capitol of the United States had burned down in the War of 1812. Eventually, in 1815, the Senate authorized the President to borrow from different local banks to fix the Capitol, White House, and cabinet quarters on their existing grounds. The Senate also bought a library in 1814 to replace the Congressional Library that was destroyed in the War of 1812.

1890

Captain Alfred Thayer Mahan was a lecturer in naval history and president of the United States Naval War College. He also published many important papers such as The Influence of Sea Power upon History, 1669-1783. He argued that British domination was largely due to their control of the seas. As such, he talked to the President of the United States and other people in charge and he focused extremely hard on the acquisition of naval bases. This was not a New phenomenon, especially with the purchase of Alaska in 1867, but Mahan's domination and true mastery of the theories of the seas gave him the power to make true change. In the 1890s, Mahan's ideas resonated with leading politicians, who made policies around his ideas. This lead to many economic victories for the United States such as the perpetual lease for a naval base at Guantanamo Bay, Cuba.

Source: Office of the Historian on Alfred Thayer Mahan →

1898

In 1898, the Spanish-American War ended Spain's colonial empire in the Western Hemisphere and secured the United States' position as a Pacific power. United States' victory against Spain produced a peace treaty that included the acquisition of Guam, Puerto Rico, and the Phillipines for the United States. The U.S. also annexed Hawai'i during the war. There was much government spending throughout the war, as would be expected, such as troops for blockades and fighting.

Source: Office of the Historian on the Spanish-American War →

1899-1913

The United States, after winning the Spanish-American War, made policies that allowed them to further their own economic interests. Some of these policies include the "Open Door" policy and the addition to the Monroe Doctrine, known as the Roosevelt Collary, that formally declares the United States' intention to use military force to defend the Western Hemisphere from the Europeans. The Panama Canal was also constructed during this time, and created many new trade opportunities previously unthinkable.

1899-1902

The United States fought in the Phillipine-American war because the Filipinos wanted independence rather than a new colonial ruler. During the war, over 4,200 Americans and 20,000 Filipinos died from combat, and as many as 200,000 Filipino citizens died from poor living conditions such as famine, disease, and violence. The decision to annex the Phillipines was not without domestic criticism either. Those who disliked the annexation of the Phillipines argued that it was immoral for the United States to engage in colonialism, but supporters countered that it would increase economic opportunity for both the United States and the Phillipines.

1917

World War One was a major part of the history of not only the United States, but the world as a whole. It caused major economic and military decisions for the countries of the world. As for the United States, President Woodrow Wilson went before Congress to request a declaration of war against Germany, citing their breaking of their pledge to suspend unrestricted submarine warfare in the North Atlantic and the Mediterranean, and their attempts to entice Mexico into an alliance against the United States. The Senate and Congress both agreed that war should be declared in early April of 1917, and later declared war on Austria-Hungary, an ally of Germany, in December of that year.

1945

In 1945, the first Atomic Bomb was tested successfully. This gave the United States a very strong lead in weapons and allowed them to use the atomic bomb as a weapon of peace, and as a bargaining tool. This did not happen for very long, as the Soviet Union exploded their first successful atomic bomb in 1949, the United Kingdom in 1952, France in 1960, and the People's Republic of China in 1964. This atomic bomb was a major reason for the Cold War between the United States and the Soviet Union, as both had atomic weapons but neither wanted to use them first. That created a policy of "Mutually Assured Destruction", where each country would destroy the other given a threat of destruction. Once these bombs became more widespread, countries such as the United States were no longer able to use them to be "the great peacemakers" and get what they wanted because they always had the pressure of a counter-attack.

Source: Office of the Historian on Atomic Diplomacy →

Truman that said that the United States would offer political, military, and economic assitance to all democratic nations under threat from in- or external authoritarian threats. This brought the United States' foreign policy from a policy of withdrawal to intervention of conflicts.

1948

The Marshall Plan was a plan implemented by Secretary of State George C. Marshall. It was a comprehensive plan to rebuild Europe post-WWII. Because of the Communist expansion and the rapid deterioration of European economies in the winter of 1946-47, Congress passed the Economic Cooperation Act in March of 1948 and approved funding that would eventually rise to over $12,000,000,000.00 for the rebuilding of Western Europe. The Marshall Plan was amazing for the United States because it created a market for U.S. goods. Eastern Europe nearly participated but Stalin shot down the idea. It might've been better for the Eastern Europe to not because Congress may not have funded the program as heavily. However, the Marshall Plan was one of the best humanitarian efforts in the history of the United States.

Source: Office of the Historian on the Marshall Plan →

1949

The North Atlantic Treaty Organization, otherwise known as NATO, was the first peacetime military alliance that the United States entered into that was outside of the Western Hemisphere. After WWII, the nations of Europe struggled to rebuild and ensure their security. Economically, the United States was in a good position to help the European nations due to their distance from the fighting of WWII and their good economic standing. The United States saw a strong Europe as the way to keep peace in the world. As such, they helped the European nations rebuild and regain their economic status. Due to this, and many other factors such as the Western European nations, Eastern Europe entered into a treaty with the each other, known as the Brussels Treaty, that ensured mutual protection. At the same time, the Truman administration increased military spending and instituted a peacetime draft. They also pressured the House to allow a military alliance with Eastern Europe. In 1948, they consented, which allowed negotiations for the North Atlantic Treaty to begin.

Source: Office of the Historian on NATO →

1955

The United States and the People's Republic of China opened a series of ambassadorial-level talks in 1955 in Geneva to discuss the repatriation of nationals and other issues of mutual concern. Because they did not have diplomatic connections, formally, the talks were the first contact in 16 years. This occurred due to the People's Republic of China bombing off-shore islands in the Taiwan Strait. The crisis ended when the Chinese Foreign Minister decided that each country should bring in ambassadors. Once it was clear that the United States would not recognize the PRC as its own country, the Chinese arrested Americans still living in China, and the United States retaliated by doing the same. Eventually, a treaty was created and the U.S. recognized the PRC, which relieved the tension between the two nations.

Source: Office of the Historian on China →

Space Race

The "Space Race" refers to a race between the United States and the Soviet Union between 1957 and ending as late as 1975 to create the first artificial satellite around the Earth. The Soviet Union was the first to launch an artificial satellite, called Sputnik-1, in 1957. The President of the United States tried to downplay the importance of this to the United States' citizens, but especially after the explosion of the Vanguard, President Eisenhower pushed even harder and funded more into the United States' space programs. Eventually, in 1958, the United States launched their firstsatellite into orbit, known as the Explorer. In many ways, the launch of the Sputnik further pushed the arms and space race to new heights.
